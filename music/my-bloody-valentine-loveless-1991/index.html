About

Darganist is where you go when you're looking for something something new and want a humans help to find it. We write about the things that move us, frustrate us, and, not infrequently, cause us to be incapacitated by overwhelming joy.

We won't write half-heartedly about things that don't matter. We won't tell you what you already know. We won't agree with prevailing opinion because it's easy and safe to do so.

We'll simply direct your attention to something we've listened to, read or watched, and ask the crucial question: on a scale of one to nuclear holocaust, how likely is this to improve your day?

title:

My Bloody Valentine

artist: Loveless

year: 1991

rating: 4

Jaymes

I remember how I first discovered My Bloody Valentine’s 1991 shoe-gaze classic Loveless. I was reading Pitchfork reviews (a late-adolescent pastime) when I saw that the magazine had listed Loveless as the best album of the 1990s. A bold claim, I thought, and, curious but unconvinced, obtained a copy posthaste.

Track one is titled ‘Only Shallow’ and this was my reaction upon hearing it for the first time:

To be fair, the genre known as ‘shoegazing’, with its distorted tsunamis of sound, can be overwhelming to the uninitiated and, frankly, pretty goddamn unpleasant. The key – though it sounds banal – is patience. Loveless is a vast sonic landscape. Its thickly layered melodies soften harsh guitar chords and occasionally wash lyrics out entirely. In fact, it’s one of the few CDs I know of whose music is perfectly represented by its album cover – blurry but bright, with almost indiscernible details:

Philip Glass once said in an interview that ‘music is a place’. This is particularly true of Loveless – it’s not music you listen to so much as inhabit. Kevin Shields, the album’s guitarist, producer and chief songwriter, has achieved a sort of musical impressionism: the closer you look, the better you can appreciate all the delicate details from which Loveless emerges.
